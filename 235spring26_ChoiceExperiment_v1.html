Welcome

Scenario: Your county is considering improvements to a popular recreation lake. Several upgrade packages are under consideration, each involving different combinations of water quality improvements, trail construction, fish stocking, and annual household fees. Your responses will help estimate the community's willingness to pay for these improvements.

You will be shown 6 choice occasions. In each, you'll see two possible improvement options (Site A and Site B) and an opt-out (keep the status quo). Choose the option you prefer most.

Multinomial Logit Model Results

The conditional logit (McFadden's choice model) estimates the probability of choosing alternative j in choice occasion t:

P(choice = j) = exp(Vj) / Σk exp(Vk) Vj = β1·WaterClarity + β2·Trails + β3·FishStocking + β4·Fee + βASC·ASC
ASC (Alternative-Specific Constant): Captures the baseline preference for choosing an improvement option over the opt-out (status quo), holding all attributes equal. A positive ASC means people are generally inclined toward action rather than inaction.

Discussion Questions

Consider these questions for class discussion:

1. Your responses were hypothetical — no real money changed hands. How might hypothetical bias affect these WTP estimates? Would you expect them to be too high or too low?

2. We estimated a conditional logit, which assumes independence of irrelevant alternatives (IIA). What does IIA mean, and when might it be violated in environmental choice experiments?

3. The fee attribute lets us calculate WTP in dollars. What if we hadn't included a monetary attribute — could we still estimate WTP? What would we lose?

4. How does this choice experiment approach compare to the travel cost method you used in Homework 3? What types of value can choice experiments capture that travel cost cannot?

5. Suppose we pooled the entire class's data. How would the sample size affect precision? What if preferences are heterogeneous — what model extension would you use?

Instructor Notes: Running This Live in Class

Option A — Individual demo (simplest): Open this HTML file directly in any browser (just double-click it or use File → Open). Walk through the intro, have a student volunteer make choices on the projector, then watch the estimation run live. Each student can also open the file on their own laptop — it's entirely self-contained, no server needed.

Option B — Share via Canvas/LMS: Upload this single HTML file to Canvas as a File or embed in an assignment page. Students download and open locally in their browser. After completing the experiment, they can click "Download Your Data (CSV)" and submit the CSV for pooled analysis.

Option C — UW Faculty Web Space (best for live classroom use): Upload this file via SFTP to your public_html directory on dante.u.washington.edu using your UW NetID. It will be served at https://faculty.washington.edu/yournetid/235spring26_ChoiceExperiment_v1.html. Share the URL on the projector or post it in Canvas — students open it on any device. (Note: a python3 -m http.server approach won't work on eduroam due to client isolation.)

Pooling class data: This demo estimates on one respondent's 6 choices (hence the ridge regularization). For a proper class exercise, have each student download their CSV, then combine all CSVs and run a standard conditional logit in R (mlogit or survival::clogit) or Python (pylogit). With 30+ students × 6 occasions = 180+ choice observations, you'll get clean estimates without regularization, and you can discuss heterogeneity, mixed logit, etc.

Teaching notes on the ASC: The ASC is included in total WTP because it captures the utility difference between "choosing to improve" vs. "doing nothing," independent of attribute levels. A positive ASC means respondents are predisposed toward action (improvement bias); a negative ASC means status-quo bias. When comparing total WTP for the full package vs. the opt-out, omitting the ASC would understate (or overstate) the welfare change. This is a great discussion point for why the outside option matters in welfare measurement.
